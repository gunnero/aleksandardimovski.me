# Editorial Style Guide

## Voice

Write in first person with a practical, direct, technical tone. Explain the decision, the constraint, the evidence, and the lesson. Prefer concrete engineering behavior over claims about seniority or enthusiasm.

## Structure

- Open with the real engineering context and its limits.
- Use descriptive headings and short paragraphs.
- Use lists only when they make a procedure or comparison easier to scan.
- Include practical examples without recreating confidential workflows.
- Close with engineering judgment rather than promotion.
- Include “What I would do differently today” when retrospective judgment is useful.

## Claims

Use exact, reviewed evidence and qualify time-bound results. Do not invent customers, revenue, users, adoption, incidents, outages, production failures, or commercial outcomes. Do not turn repository test counts into claims of complete correctness.

## Technology language

Do not frame technologies as winners and losers. Explain why a stack fit a product constraint. Keep Aleksandar’s established PHP and Laravel experience distinct from current Python and FastAPI product work. Never infer years of experience from project activity.

## Readability

Articles should be 1,200–1,800 words, use a comfortable reading measure, preserve heading hierarchy, and avoid dense text walls. Links should be contextual and restrained. Code blocks must be necessary, readable in both themes, and free of secrets or private topology.

## Prohibited filler

Avoid generic openings, fake quotes, exaggerated claims, marketing language, “in today’s fast-paced world,” and “passionate developer.” Every paragraph should add evidence, reasoning, or a useful limitation.
